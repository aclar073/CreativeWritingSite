Untitled

Written by Amanda Clark

During the day, the house is quite unassuming. The shutters hang haphazardly from the darkened windows. The porch sags in the middle as if tired of remaining upright. There is paint peeling from every exterior surface. Neighbors passed by without a second glance at the overgrown yard riddled with weeds, thankful that their properties appeared nicer by comparison. Children played ball in the cul-de-sac but never ventured particularly close to the seemingly abandoned home. During the day, the house, as unsightly as it was, simply blended into the suburban scenery.

When adults were out of earshot, the children often joked that the house was haunted. They had been scolded enough by their parents and learned quickly to say such things no louder than a whisper. They spoke of strange shadows in the windows after dark and inhuman noises emanating from the upper portion of the home. The adults noticed this as well but did not dare admit it.

Mr. Jones had lived in the house for as long as he could remember. His memory was questionable these days at the age of eighty-five, but he did remember running the halls as a child. His mother would scold him for running in the house. Now he struggled to shuffle with his walker and maintained a slow but steady pace. This would have pleased his mother. He wished she was here to see him.

She left him the house in her will. He assumed she intended him to raise his own children here, but Mr. Jones had never married. He had no interest in a relationship and found children to be quite annoying. Their loud shrieks used to express a variety of emotions left his ears ringing even as a younger man. He was content with his life of solitude. He found it comforting not having to answer another person and having the freedom to do as he pleases. He seldom left his home during the day and the neighbors had seen very little of him. Even less as time has passed, his previously sinewy frame atrophied and skeletal.

The only person he saw regularly was Mrs. Wilson. She was his home health nurse and came weekly. He assumed it was to make sure he hadn’t died but did not verbalize this assumption. Her feelings would surely be hurt, and he did not like it when she felt strongly about much of anything.

When the doorbell rang Mr. Jones was quite surprised. Mrs. Wilson’s schedule was like clockwork. She showed up at 1 pm, no sooner and no later. It was a quarter past 9 am and Mrs. Wilson was not due for another two days. He was not expecting a visitor and pondered if one of the children worked up the nerve to ring the bell and run away. Lost in thought, Mr. Jones jumped at the sound of the doorbell ringing yet again. At this point he was annoyed by this intrusion into his normally peaceful Monday morning.

He slowly shuffled his way towards the front door and the doorbell rang for a third time. He grunted as the tire of his walker caught on the table of the foyer. He cautiously pulled it backwards and stumbled as it unstuck. He caught himself against the wall and felt the vast number of framed pictures rattle at the impact. As he finally reached the door, he found his legs to be weak at the exertion of maneuvering from the kitchen.

He opened the door and did not see Mrs. Wilson, or the previously expected empty doorstep caused by an unrulily child. Instead, he saw a man he did not recognize. His eyebrows knit together in confusion and concern as he inspected this uninvited stranger at his door.

This stranger was indeed strange. He carried a battered leather briefcase that looked as if it were new sometime in the 1950’s. In fact, everything about this man appeared to come from the 1950’s. He wore a pair of charcoal gray high waisted trousers, a white dress shirt, and a matching charcoal gray topcoat. His light brown hair was parted to the side and held in place with some form of gel. To Mr. Jones, it appeared as if this man stepped out of the past and landed on his front porch. As he performed his visual inspection of the stranger, he was meet with the confident grin of a businessman.

Mr. Jones did not like this man and wished he would leave.

“What are you trying to sell me?” Mr. Jones asked curtly, hoping his disapproval was evident in his gravelly voice.

“Why, Mr. Jones, I am not trying to see you anything. I have a proposal for you, free of charge, I think you may appreciate.”

How does this kook know my name? Mr. Jones thought to himself. He was caught off guard by this and simply grunted at the man. He considered closing the door and ending the conversation then.

About the Story

This is an incomplete work of fiction. I had no particular inspiration or intentions with this story. The only thing on my mind when writing this piece was a creepy old house. I hope to finish the story when I have more of a concrete direction for the plot.

I think this piece has a lot of potential. I enjoyed using sensory description to paint the picture of a decaying and neglected home. I also used this piece as an attempt at writing in the third-person omniscient point-of-view.
